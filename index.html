The Undue Hardship Standard

A reference on the legal standard for discharging student loans in bankruptcy under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8). The Brunner test, the totality-of-circumstances alternative, and how the standard varies by circuit.

The statutory hook

Federal student loans (and certain other education loans) are presumptively non-dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8), except where excepting them from discharge would impose an “undue hardship” on the debtor and the debtor’s dependents. The statute does not define undue hardship. Courts have built the standard through case law.

“A discharge under section 727, 1141, 1192, 1228(a), 1228(b), or 1328(b) of this title does not discharge an individual debtor from any debt — ... (8) unless excepting such debt from discharge under this paragraph would impose an undue hardship on the debtor and the debtor’s dependents, for — [federal student loans, qualified educational loans, etc.]” — 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8)

The Brunner test

The most widely applied test for undue hardship comes from Brunner v. New York State Higher Education Services Corp., 831 F.2d 395 (2d Cir. 1987). Brunner adopted a three-part standard, and the debtor bears the burden of proving all three elements:

1. Minimal standard of living

The debtor cannot maintain, based on current income and expenses, a “minimal” standard of living for themselves and any dependents if forced to repay the loans. Courts examine actual budgets, not aspirational ones — the question is whether basic subsistence (housing, food, transportation, medical care) is genuinely unaffordable while servicing the loan.

2. Persistence of the hardship

Additional circumstances exist indicating that the inability to repay is likely to persist for a significant portion of the loan repayment period. Temporary financial setbacks generally do not satisfy this prong; courts look for evidence of long-term constraints (disability, age, limited earning capacity, ongoing care obligations).

3. Good-faith repayment efforts

The debtor has made good-faith efforts to repay the loans. Courts evaluate whether the debtor explored reasonable alternatives — deferment, forbearance, income-driven repayment plans, attempts to maximize income — before seeking discharge.

The Brunner test has been adopted, in some form, by the majority of federal circuits.

The totality-of-circumstances alternative

The Eighth Circuit and the First Circuit apply a less rigid “totality of the circumstances” approach rather than the three-part Brunner framework. The totality test takes into account:

  • The debtor’s past, present, and reasonably reliable future financial resources;
  • A calculation of the debtor’s and dependents’ reasonable necessary living expenses; and
  • Any other relevant facts and circumstances surrounding the debtor’s particular situation.

The Eighth Circuit articulated this standard in In re Long, 322 F.3d 549 (8th Cir. 2003). The First Circuit follows a similar approach.

Within each Brunner-adopting circuit, the application of the three prongs varies considerably. Some courts apply Brunner with strict rigor; others apply it more flexibly. Recent post-2022 DOJ-guidance decisions have, in many cases, applied Brunner with greater attention to the totality of debtor circumstances even within Brunner’s formal three-part structure.

The November 2022 DOJ guidance

On November 17, 2022, the Department of Justice and Department of Education issued a joint guidance memorandum revising how the U.S. responds to undue-hardship adversary proceedings involving federal student loans. Under the guidance, DOJ attorneys use a streamlined attestation form completed by the debtor and apply internal criteria to decide whether to stipulate to discharge or contest the AP. Where DOJ does not contest, courts may grant discharge based on stipulation rather than fully litigated factual findings.

The guidance is administrative; it does not change § 523(a)(8) or the Brunner/totality case law. But it has materially changed the procedural landscape: the Open Bankruptcy Project's empirical research shows roughly a sevenfold increase in § 523(a)(8) AP filings against the U.S. Department of Education following the guidance.

Important limits on this page

This page describes general law — not your case. Whether the undue-hardship standard is met in any specific situation depends on the facts of that case, the controlling circuit precedent, and the application of that precedent by the assigned judge. Courts have reached different conclusions on similar-sounding facts.

The cases cited on this page (Brunner, In re Long) are listed for reference. Citing them in a filing requires confirming the current authority of each citation in the relevant jurisdiction and reading the original opinions in full. Outcomes of post-2022 § 523(a)(8) APs are an active area of legal development; precedents continue to evolve.
